# RSI Research Master Summary

## Project Objective

The original objective of this project was to determine whether the Relative Strength Index (RSI) could identify repeatable market inefficiencies across multiple asset classes.

The project began with the expectation that strong trading strategies would produce very high win rates while generating large numbers of opportunities.

The research ultimately led to a different conclusion.

The goal shifted from finding a perfect strategy to identifying repeatable, evidence-based market inefficiencies.

# Major Findings

## Finding #1

### The Market Matters More Than The Indicator

The same RSI indicator produced dramatically different results depending on the asset.

Examples:

* Gold produced strong bullish mean-reversion behavior.
* NAS100 produced strong buy-the-dip behavior.
* SP500 produced strong buy-the-dip behavior.
* USDCHF produced meaningful bearish mean-reversion behavior.
* GBPUSD produced moderate bearish asymmetry.
* EURUSD produced mostly random behavior.
* USDJPY produced little meaningful edge.

### Core Lesson

> The market matters more than the indicator.

An indicator is a measuring device.

An edge is the interaction between an indicator and a specific market.

---

## Finding #2

### RSI Works On Some Assets And Fails On Others

The research demonstrated that RSI is not universally effective.

Examples:

* Gold RSI Alone produced approximately 63% bullish win rates.
* USDCHF RSI Alone produced approximately 55%-57% win rates.
* NAS100 RSI Alone produced approximately 57% bullish win rates.
* USDJPY RSI Alone produced approximately 50% win rates.

### Core Lesson

> RSI Alone is not automatically an edge.

RSI works on some assets and fails on others.

---

## Finding #3

### Long And Short Edges Are Often Not Symmetrical

Many assets displayed significant differences between bullish and bearish performance.

Examples:

* Gold favored bullish reversals.
* Silver favored bullish reversals.
* NAS100 favored bullish reversals.
* SP500 favored bullish reversals.
* GBPUSD favored bearish reversals.
* USDCHF favored bearish reversals.

### Core Lesson

> Long and short edges should be evaluated independently.

---

## Finding #4

### Opportunity Frequency Matters

Several assets produced strong win rates but very few opportunities.

Examples:

* Silver generated only 48 oversold signals.
* NAS100 Weekly Structure generated only 12 bullish signals.
* Gold structure-based filters significantly reduced opportunities.

### Core Lesson

> An edge should be evaluated by both its strength and its frequency.

High win rates alone do not create practical strategies.

---

## Finding #5

### Most Filters Reduced Opportunities More Than They Improved Win Rates

Throughout the project, confirmation filters frequently:

* Reduced signal count dramatically.
* Produced little improvement in win rates.
* Sometimes reduced performance outright.

Examples:

* Daily Structure
* Weekly Structure
* Double Bottom / Top
* Inside Bar

### Core Lesson

> Most filters tested so far have reduced opportunity count far more than they increased win rate.

---

## Finding #6

### Location Appears To Matter More Than Pattern Recognition

Location-based filters occasionally improved results.

Pattern-based filters often reduced opportunity count without materially improving performance.

Examples:

* Gold near Daily Low improved performance.
* EURUSD Daily Structure outperformed Double Bottoms.
* Many Double Bottom and Inside Bar tests produced only marginal improvements.

### Core Lesson

> Location appears to matter more than pattern recognition.

---

## Finding #7

### Weekly Structure Was Usually Too Restrictive

Across multiple assets:

* Weekly Structure dramatically reduced signal counts.
* Win-rate improvements were inconsistent.
* Results often became statistically fragile.

### Core Lesson

> Daily Structure is close enough to influence H4 reversals, while Weekly Structure may be too distant and too coarse.

---

## Finding #8

### Strong Markets Rarely Revisit Important Lows

This was most obvious in NAS100.

Examples:

* NAS100 RSI Oversold Signals: 175
* NAS100 Daily Structure Signals: 20
* NAS100 Weekly Structure Signals: 12

### Core Lesson

> A market can be so strongly biased in one direction that meaningful pullbacks become rare events.

---

## Finding #9

### Do Not Over-Filter Strong Bullish Assets

Gold, Silver, SP500, and NAS100 repeatedly showed bullish asymmetry.

Additional filters often improved headline statistics while destroying most opportunities.

### Core Lesson

> Don't over-filter a market that already wants to go up.

---

## Finding #10

### Winning Strategies Lose Frequently

One of the most important discoveries of the project.

Many of the strongest strategies still lost:

* 37%
* 40%
* 45%

of the time.

### Core Lesson

> The goal is not to eliminate losses.

> The goal is to ensure the wins outweigh the losses over a large sample.

The market does not pay traders for being right all the time.

The market pays traders for being right often enough while managing risk.

---

# Final Project Conclusion

At the beginning of the project, the objective was to discover a high-win-rate strategy capable of generating thousands of opportunities.

As the research expanded across multiple assets and strategies, a different reality emerged.

The most robust market inefficiencies consistently appeared in the high-50% to low-60% range.

The project shifted away from finding a perfect strategy and toward building a portfolio of verified market inefficiencies.

The objective became:

* Identify repeatable edges.
* Measure edge strength.
* Measure opportunity frequency.
* Understand market behavior.
* Build a portfolio of evidence-based strategies.

### Final Lesson

> Don't fall in love with indicators.

> Fall in love with evidence.
